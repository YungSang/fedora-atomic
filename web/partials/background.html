Packages, Images, and Trees

Traditional GNU/Linux distributions come as a collection of packages, which one can assemble in many ways. They typically target a variety of hardware, offer choices of things like filesystems. A weakness of this model is that there are many system configurations which go untested, or nearly so. It's very easy for upgraded systems to diverge from fresh installations, due to the way package managers upgrade piecemeal.

At the other end of the spectrum, systems like ChromeOS and CoreOS come pre-assembled, and target fixed use cases. Their update systems reflect this targeting - they're not designed to allow much flexibility on the client side.

OSTree allows a middle ground - one can assemble multiple trees from a variety of packages on a build server, naming the resulting filesystem trees that are the combination of packages, and then clients can reliablly replicate and upgrade. Furthermore, client systems can switch between these pre-assembled trees.

Short Term Goal - Testing Rawhide

The Atomic Initiative will track Fedora Rawhide, assembling packages into filesystem trees, and offering them for atomic replication by both human and machine consumers.

Why machine consumers? The very same rpm-ostree codebase actually contains qemu-kvm based testing infrastructure. It's designed to rapidly boot a new updated tree as a virtual guest, running arbitrary testing code, and pulling out telemetry such as log files for display.

Concretely, the build-status results are live - the screenshots will change as the packages change, automatically.

Short Term Goal - Testing OSTree

While the core OSTree codebase is now fairly stable, rpm-ostree is undergoing rapid evolution. It's only recently that SELinux is fully enforcing, for example. The goal is for everything to be packaged, and to become a more integrated part of the Fedora Infrastructure.
